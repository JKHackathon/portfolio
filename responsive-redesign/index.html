Responsive Redesign

How many times have you looked at a webpage and felt aghast at the confusing user interface? Have you ever thought about how bad the accessibility of a webpage might be? Maybe you’ve pulled up a webpage on your phone and discovered that half the page is cut off and the buttons are in the wrong place. Well lucky for you, responsive redesign is meant to address all these concerns!

In Search of Trouble

When redesigning a webpage, we must first find a webpage that needs a redesign. So I scoured the internet for an interesting website and stumbled upon this article on penguins on a scientific blog:

This article is not responsive at all, like many other older scientific blogs. It is a cute little website, but it has not been updated since 2015 and is lacking in many ways. But first, let’s talk about what we like. All the text has a high enough contrast to be legible, the grouping on the page is pretty clear, and the layout generally guides your eyes towards the important information.

Now for what we can improve. The title of the article reads almost like normal text as it is just slightly larger, while the subheaders are at least bolded, though the size should clearly separate them as well. There is also little distinction between the image captions and the main body. Strangely, despite the font choice being legible, the actual readability is not great, perhaps due to its light weight. Since grouping is primarily established through borders and dividers, the website feels a little old.

There are also some problems that are less apparent. A number of images are missing alternative text, which allows for screen readers to describe what an image looks like. The links on the sidebar, such as in the “Watch Video” category, are also confusing for screen readers as the image and text are linked separately, resulting in difficulty navigating and understanding the structure of the webpage. Links in the sidebar also are not obviously clickable as there is no hover effect. Contrast in the navigation bar is also extremely low, making it difficult for even average users.

And of course, the biggest issue: the webpage is completely unresponsive. This means that if you resize your window or use a different device, information will be cut off.

Visual Redesign

Whenever coming up with a design, I always start with some simple sketches to visualize how changes might look. After settling on a more tame sketch, I created a rudimentary wireframe for different devices that addressed most of the little issues we noticed. I made changes like removing borders between groups, italicizing captions, enlarging and bolding headers, creating cards to hold images and text for the sidebar links.

Sidebars are always a bit of a challenge when it comes to mobile devices, as there are a lot of different options. I opted to move all the sidebar content below the main article as I felt this matched a lot of well-known existing designs. Ultimately, I wanted to maintain the general style of the original webpage as it has a little charm to it, but it needed to feel a little more modern and also have a more clear and consistent layout.

Once I felt decently satisfied, I moved to a high-fidelity mockup, which would appear more like the finished product with all the colors and styling and logos added in, while still allowing for changes to be quickly made. I created a color palette based on the colors of a penguin, using a very dark blue for the main text and adding a couple of accent colors lacking in the original website, in order to more clearly highlight text. With this color palette and some new fonts in hand, I was ready.
